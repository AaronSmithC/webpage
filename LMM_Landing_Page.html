The Problem

Rehabilitation is high-stakes,
yet largely unaided by AI

Physical therapists make consequential clinical decisions every day ‚Äî assessing recovery, adjusting treatment, identifying compensatory movement ‚Äî almost entirely through visual observation. Objective motion data exists only in well-resourced academic labs.

Technical Approach

Motion as a sequence domain

LMM applies the core insight behind large language models ‚Äî that sequence-learning architectures discover generalizable structure from consistent, well-curated data ‚Äî to the temporal evolution of human joint positions.

1

Pose Estimation

Video inputs are processed through OpenPose, MMPose, and VIBE to extract BODY_25 keypoints ‚Äî 25 joints covering the full clinically relevant kinematic chain.

2

Normalization

Skeletons are centered on the hip midpoint, scaled to standardized torso length, and resampled to a uniform frame rate. Velocity and acceleration are computed as derived features.

3

Quality Control & Metadata

Each sequence receives QC metadata: per-joint confidence scores, occlusion masks, artifact detection, domain tags, and MoE-routing preparation fields.

4

Tokenization

Three schemes are evaluated in parallel: per-frame tokens, windowed tokens (3‚Äì5 frames), and body-part tokens (upper, lower, core). Fidelity vs. efficiency is compared across all three.

5

Model Training & Evaluation

Transformer and diffusion model baselines are trained on the normalized corpus. Evaluation spans quantitative accuracy, biomechanical validity, and correlation with clinician-assigned quality scores.

Tokenization Strategies
Per-Frame Token
Full 25-joint pose at each timestep. High fidelity, larger sequence length. Direct representation of instantaneous posture.
Windowed Token (3‚Äì5 frames)
Short temporal windows compressed into a single token. Captures short-range motion dynamics; reduces sequence length for transformer context.
Body-Part Token
Upper limb, lower limb, and core processed as separate streams. Enables specialized attention to clinically meaningful body regions.
Data Foundation

Curated public rehabilitation datasets

Phase I draws on five complementary public motion datasets, unified under a shared BODY_25 schema. Together they provide coverage of functional exercises, quality annotations, pathological movement, and large-scale pretraining signal.

UI-PRMD

Ten common PT exercises captured with Vicon and Kinect systems. Designed specifically for rehabilitation AI research. Provides Vicon ground truth for biomechanical validation.

Exercise & ROM

KiMoRe

Rehabilitation exercises with clinician-assigned quality scores ‚Äî a rare source of supervised signal linking motion data directly to clinical assessment.

Clinical Quality Scores

KIMORE

Extension of KiMoRe including both healthy and pathological movement populations, supporting evaluation across the clinical spectrum.

Pathological Movement

PhysioNet

Pathological gait collections including Parkinson's and Huntington's disease ‚Äî directly representative of PT patient populations and neurodegenerative conditions.

Gait & Neurological

AMASS

Aggregation of 15+ motion capture datasets totaling approximately 40 hours of motion. Used for large-scale pretraining to establish robust priors before rehabilitation fine-tuning.

Evaluation Framework

Three levels of model validation

Evaluation is designed to bridge engineering performance and clinical relevance ‚Äî not just model accuracy, but biomechanical plausibility and alignment with how clinicians actually assess movement quality.

üìê

Quantitative Metrics

  • Mean squared error (MSE) on held-out sequences
  • Smoothness: mean jerk across predicted trajectories
  • Temporal consistency over long autoregressive rollouts
ü¶¥

Biomechanical Validity

  • Joint-limit violation rate (physiological ROM bounds)
  • Center-of-mass continuity across frames
  • Bilateral symmetry indices for gait sequences
üè•

Clinical Alignment

  • Correlation with KiMoRe clinician-assigned scores
  • Movement quality scoring across exercise types
  • Primary bridge between model output and clinical relevance
