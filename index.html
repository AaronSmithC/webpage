About Aegis Station

Aegis Station is a civilian orbital infrastructure platform—modular, shielded, and engineered for long-duration life and operations in lunar orbit.

But Aegis is more than a habitat. It is an integrated system: water tankers, surface rovers, shuttles, and interorbital transports working together to move mass from the Moon into stable orbit. The station is the hub—but the logistics chain is the backbone.

Designed around proven technologies and near-term deployment pathways, Aegis prioritizes shielding, transport, and operational permanence first—enabling habitation, research, industry, and commerce to follow.

Our Approach

Projects at the scale of Aegis Station exceed the practical scope of any single fabrication shop. They require coordinated development across propulsion, structures, life support, autonomy, logistics, and orbital systems.

For that reason, Aegis operates under a subcontractor-first model. Domain experts design and build individual systems, while centralized architectural authority and program management preserve design intent, control interfaces, manage integration risk, and maintain schedule discipline.

Implementation remains flexible. Architectural intent does not.

Why We Updated to 1g

Aegis Station was originally designed with 0.5g artificial gravity—enough to reduce health risks in orbit while easing structural demands. But as our vision matured and long-term habitation took center stage, we developed a 1g variant using the same core architecture.

The change nearly doubles habitable capacity without doubling cost. It also offers full Earth gravity for crew health, physical performance, and compatibility with terrestrial biological systems. Most supporting systems—shuttles, rovers, tankers—remain unchanged.

Aegis-Class Rover

Aegis-Class Lunar Surface Mobility Platform

The Aegis-Class Rover is a pressurized, long-duration lunar surface vehicle engineered for 30–60 day missions in polar terrain. Designed as infrastructure—not a short-sortie explorer—it combines habitation, mobility, dust boundary control, and integrated power/thermal architecture in a single wide-track platform.

  • Crew: 2–4 (up to 6 emergency capacity)
  • Mission Duration: 30–60 days autonomous
  • Gross Vehicle Mass: ~5,400 kg (laden)
  • Track Width: 4.0 m • Static Stability Factor ~1.30
  • Habitable Volume: ~57 m³ gross
  • Suitport-based EVA with dust isolation boundary
  • Integrated stacked solar-shade radiator architecture (SSSRA)

Mission Profile

  • Polar water prospecting and ISRU support
  • Surface-to-site crew transport
  • Mobile command and field operations platform
  • Solar storm shelter and emergency refuge

Geometry is driven from interior standing height outward—cabin height sets hull dimensions, which define CG, which defines track width and stability margin. The roof footprint then governs radiator and solar array sizing for polar sun angles.

Systems Summary

  • Multi-axle independent suspension with wide-track stability
  • LIDAR + visual SLAM navigation with autonomous modes
  • Reduced-pressure cabin (8.0 psia) optimized for EVA cadence
  • Battery storage with deployable solar augmentation and fuel cell backup
  • Heading-independent thermal rejection via roof-integrated SSSRA

Luna–Aegis Shuttle

The "Short Hopper" – Linking Surface and Station

The Luna–Aegis Shuttle is a reusable single-stage VTOL lunar ferry connecting Aegis Station in low lunar orbit with surface sites near the Moon's south pole. Designed for crew, cargo, and hybrid missions, it carries up to 4 astronauts or 1,000 kg of cargo across a 1,500–2,000 km range — with direct pressurised docking to the Aegis-Class Rover, no EVA required.

  • Vertical takeoff and landing (VTOL) on unprepared lunar terrain
  • 4 crew standard (6 max); up to 1,000 kg cargo in cargo config
  • Single gimbaled LOX/LH₂ vacuum engine — ~25–30 kN, Isp 430–450 s
  • Gross wet mass ~8,000 kg; design ΔV 1,800 m/s (10% margin)
  • Direct pressurised rover docking — no EVA required for crew transfer
  • Fully autonomous landing with ±3 m precision (LUNET beacon compatible)

Operational Profile

  • Surface-to-orbit crew and cargo transfer; ISRU-compatible LOX/LH₂ propellants
  • Intra-lunar hops up to 2,000 km — supports outpost resupply, sample return, and medevac
  • 24–48 hr turnaround with LUNET node support; min. 5–10 sorties per vehicle
  • Fully integrated with Aegis Station, Aegis-Class Rover, and LUNET beacon network

Earth–Aegis Long-Hauler

The Orbital Railroad — Cargo and Crew, Scaled for Tomorrow

The Long-Hauler is a spacefaring freightliner built to bridge Earth and Aegis Station. Designed with no launch constraints, it moves people and cargo like an orbital train: modular, scalable, and grounded in current technology. Its forward crew and passenger cars carry up to 48 people comfortably, while trailing freight modules can haul tanks, habitats, RONs, or regolith processors.

  • Zero-g-native linear configuration
  • Forward crew command pod + stackable passenger cars
  • Rear-mounted propulsion and power blocks
  • Up to 48 passengers and 30 tons of cargo
  • Compatible with Aegis Station and surface-bound shuttles

AOCN: Orbital Compute Spine

The Aegis Orbital Compute Node (AOCN) is a modular LEO compute platform built around current launch, power, thermal, and communications technologies. It's not a cloud replacement — it's infrastructure for workloads where heat rejection and serviceability set the scale.

  • Radiator Area: 1,800–3,300 m²/MW (dominant thermal driver; 290–330 K operating range)
  • Solar Arrays: ~2,500 m²/MW — co-equal structural driver alongside radiators
  • Power: MW-class arrays with eclipse buffering or compute throttling during shadow
  • Comms: Optical backbone + Ka-band RF fallback
  • Design focus: Thermal realism, modular servicing, shielded compute core
